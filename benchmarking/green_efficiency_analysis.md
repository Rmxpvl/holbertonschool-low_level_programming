## Measurement Methodology

In this project, we conducted controlled experiments to measure the execution times of predefined algorithms and multi-phase programs in C. All programs were compiled using GCC with the flags -Wall -Werror -Wextra -pedantic -std=gnu89 to ensure strict compliance with ISO C90 standards. Timing was measured using the clock() function from <time.h>, which reports CPU time consumed by the program. For the comparison of two algorithms, count_even_naive() and count_even_single_pass(), each program was executed three times, and the execution times were recorded to calculate the average runtime. Similarly, for the multi-phase program, instrumentation_lab.c, each phase—dataset building, processing, and checksum reduction—was measured independently using separate clock() calls to obtain precise per-phase execution times, as well as a total runtime. This method ensures reproducibility and allows comparison across implementations while controlling for environmental factors such as operating system scheduling and background processes.

## Observed Performance Differences

The execution times for the two algorithm implementations highlighted a substantial difference in efficiency. The naive algorithm, count_even_naive(), averaged approximately 1.345 seconds, while the single-pass algorithm, count_even_single_pass(), averaged 0.000054 seconds over three runs. This indicates that the naive implementation is roughly 24,743 times slower than the single-pass approach. The significant difference is attributed to the nested loop structure in the naive algorithm (O(n²) complexity) versus the single-pass linear iteration (O(n) complexity). In the multi-phase program, the measured average times for each phase reflected the computational intensity of the operations. The dataset building phase, which involves generating pseudo-random numbers, typically consumes more time than simple arithmetic processing. The checksum reduction phase, relying on a sequential accumulation over all elements, also contributes noticeably to the total runtime.

## Relation Between Runtime and Energy Consumption

Runtime serves as a proxy for energy consumption: longer CPU activity generally translates to higher energy usage. The naive algorithm, with its O(n²) complexity, not only increases runtime dramatically but would also lead to proportionally greater CPU power usage in real-world applications. The single-pass algorithm demonstrates that optimizing the algorithmic structure reduces both runtime and implied energy expenditure. In the multi-phase program, the total runtime is always greater than or equal to the sum of individual phases due to measurement boundaries, but each phase’s contribution can be identified. Minimizing expensive operations in frequently executed phases directly lowers cumulative CPU activity and, by extension, energy consumption.

## Limitations of the Experiment

Several limitations should be considered. Firstly, clock() measures CPU time rather than actual energy consumption in watts, meaning environmental power factors and hardware-specific efficiency are not captured. Secondly, minor variations in runtime can occur between executions due to background processes or OS scheduling, despite multiple measurements. Thirdly, dataset sizes were fixed, and results may not generalize linearly for much larger inputs or multi-threaded executions. Finally, compiler optimizations and caching behavior can subtly affect timing outcomes, though the strict compilation flags help mitigate these factors.

## Practical Engineering Takeaway

The project reinforces the principle that algorithmic efficiency is critical for sustainable software design. Empirical measurement using controlled experiments enables engineers to make evidence-based decisions rather than relying on intuition. Even small inefficiencies in commonly executed loops can magnify CPU usage and energy costs at scale. By identifying performance bottlenecks and choosing linear or optimized approaches over naive implementations, developers can produce software that is both faster and more energy-efficient. Furthermore, disciplined instrumentation, including per-phase timing, supports transparency and reproducibility, which are essential for professional engineering practice.
